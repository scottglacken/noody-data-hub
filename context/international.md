## The Three Phases of International Expansion

### Phase 1: Validate (Proof, Not Profit)
- Goal: Prove that a market will buy your product
- Ship from home country, accept lower margins, don't invest in local infrastructure
- Run small ad tests ($50-100/day) to 3-5 target countries
- Track: orders, CPA, AOV, return rate, shipping complaints
- **Decision criteria:** Are people buying? Is CPA within 2x your home market? Is there repeat behavior?
- Duration: 4-8 weeks per market

### Phase 2: Scale (Maximize Profit)
- Goal: Grow the validated market to meaningful revenue
- Break out the market as its own Ecommerce Equation sheet (separate tracking of revenue, ad spend, profit)
- Ramp ad spend gradually (the 6-week sprint approach)
- Consider local fulfillment (3PL) once volume justifies it
- Track: daily profit by region, MER by region, contribution margin
- **This is where most of the growth happens** — dedicated focus and clarity

### Phase 3: Invest (Efficiency)
- Goal: Optimize the now-established market for maximum efficiency
- Local stock, optimized shipping, potentially local team
- Prorate fixed costs across regions based on revenue share
- Full integration into business planning and forecasting

## Market Selection

### Signals of a Good Market
- Existing organic orders (Shopify shipping country data)
- Cultural/language fit (English-speaking markets are easiest first)
- Reasonable shipping costs and transit times
- Favorable tariff/duty environment
- Large addressable market relative to competition

### Recommended Expansion Order (for NZ/AU brands)
1. **Australia** — closest, similar culture, easy logistics
2. **USA** — largest market, highest upside, more complex (tariffs, 3PL, sales tax)
3. **UK/Europe** — strong demand but VAT/customs complexity
4. **Rest of world** — case by case based on organic signals

## Pricing for International Markets

### Purchasing Power Parity (The Big Mac Index Principle)
Don't just convert your home currency price to the target currency. Consider what locals are willing to pay.

**Rule of thumb:** Mark up 10-15% above the raw exchange rate conversion.
- Covers currency fluctuation risk
- Accounts for additional shipping/logistics costs
- Consumers in larger markets (US, UK) often have higher willingness to pay
- Test pricing — you may be able to charge more than you think

### Currency Display
- Always show prices in local currency on the storefront
- Use Shopify Markets or a multi-currency app
- Round to clean numbers in the local currency (e.g., $59 USD not $58.73)

### Promotional Strategy by Market
- Seasonal calendars differ (summer/winter flipped for hemispheres)
- Key shopping events vary: BFCM is global, but Mother's Day, back-to-school, etc. differ by country
- Build a separate promotional calendar for each major market
- Don't assume what works in your home market transfers — test locally

## Operations & Logistics

### 3PL vs Ship from Home — Decision Framework

| Factor | Ship from Home | 3PL in Market |
|--------|---------------|---------------|
| SKU count <100 | Works fine | Ideal at scale |
| SKU count 100-500 | Manageable | Good fit |
| SKU count 500+ | Very difficult | Almost never works well |
| Orders <350/month | More economical | Overhead too high |
| Orders 350+/month | Time-consuming | Crossover point — 3PL wins |
| Transit time | 5-15 days international | 2-5 days domestic |
| Customer experience | Slower, tracking gaps | Fast, local tracking |
| Returns | Expensive to return internationally | Local returns address |

### 3PL Cost Components (Often Hidden)
- **Storage fees**: Per pallet/shelf per month
- **Pick and pack fees**: Per order + per item
- **Receiving fees**: Per unit when stock arrives
- **Minimum monthly fees**: Even if volume is low
- **Integration fees**: Connecting to Shopify
- **Returns processing**: Per return handled

**Action:** Get full cost breakdown before committing. Calculate total cost per order at your expected volume and compare to shipping from home.

### The 3PL Calculator
Total monthly 3PL cost = Storage + (Pick & Pack x Orders) + Receiving + Minimums + Returns
Compare to: Current international shipping cost x Orders + your time value

### Shipping Strategy
- Offer tracked shipping (even if more expensive) — reduces "where is my order?" support load
- Consider DDP (Delivered Duty Paid) — customer pays no surprise fees at delivery
- Free shipping threshold should be set per market based on local AOV
- Display estimated delivery times clearly on product pages

## USA-Specific Considerations

### Tariffs & De Minimis (2025-2026)
- **De minimis threshold removed** for many product categories as of 2025
- Previously, shipments under $800 USD entered duty-free — this is changing
- Products now subject to tariff regardless of value (varies by HS code)
- **Postal route vs commercial route**: Different tariff treatment
  - Postal (via national post): May have different/delayed enforcement
  - Commercial (via courier/3PL): Full tariff applies immediately

### Zonos Integration
- Zonos calculates and collects duties/taxes at checkout (DDP model)
- Customer sees total landed cost upfront — no surprise charges at delivery
- Requires verified Zonos account and correct HS code classification
- **HS Codes**: Get these right — incorrect codes = wrong duty rates or customs delays
- Zonos handles: duty calculation, tax collection, customs documentation

### US Sales Tax
- Each state has its own threshold (typically ~$100K revenue or 200 transactions)
- Once exceeded, you must collect and remit sales tax in that state
- **Strategy insight:** Broad geographic targeting (not focusing on one state) naturally spreads orders across states, keeping you under thresholds longer
- Use automated sales tax tools (TaxJar, Avalara) once you trigger nexus

### US Ad Targeting
- Do NOT restrict ads to specific states — let the algorithm optimize
- Data shows purchases come from unexpected states (Montana, Connecticut, Virginia)
- Restricting to obvious states (CA, NY, FL) inflates CPMs due to competition
- Broad targeting is consistently more efficient and reaches untapped demand

## Tracking International Performance

### Separate Ecommerce Equation Per Region
The most impactful action for scaling internationally:

1. **Duplicate your Clarity Sheet** for each target market
2. **Revenue**: Filter Shopify orders by shipping/billing country
3. **Ad spend**: Use Meta/Google breakdown by delivery country
4. **Variable costs**: Adjust for international shipping, duties, currency conversion fees
5. **Fixed costs**: Initially allocate zero FC to the new market (let the home market carry FC). As the market grows and becomes material (>20% of revenue), start prorating FC.

### Key Metrics to Track Per Market
| Metric | What to Watch |
|--------|--------------|
| Daily revenue | Is it growing week over week? |
| MER | Is acquisition cost acceptable for this market? |
| AOV | Often different from home market — adjust expectations |
| Shipping cost % | Higher internationally — monitor closely |
| Return rate | International returns are expensive — flag early |
| Delivery complaints | Transit time issues signal logistics problems |
| Contribution margin | Must be positive before scaling further |

### The 6-Week Sprint for New Markets
Apply the same 6-Week Game Plan framework to international expansion:
- **Week 1-2:** Set up tracking, establish baseline data, validate demand signal
- **Week 3-4:** Ramp ad spend gradually, test creative for local market
- **Week 5-6:** Evaluate profit — if positive, lock in and continue scaling
- **Post-sprint:** If profitable, make it a permanent part of the business. If not, reduce to maintenance level or exit.

## Common International Expansion Mistakes

1. **Scaling without tracking profit by region** — you can't improve what you can't see
2. **Focusing on ROAS instead of regional profit** — international shipping costs can eat ROAS-profitable orders
3. **Ignoring customs/duties** — surprise charges at delivery kill customer experience and drive returns
4. **Underestimating shipping costs** — get real quotes, not estimates
5. **Copying home market pricing** — adjust for purchasing power and local competition
6. **Restricting ad targeting to obvious locations** — let the algorithm find demand
7. **Trying to do everything at once** — validate one market at a time, then scale
